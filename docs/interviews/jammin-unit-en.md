**Jammin Unit** (Martin Schopf) is a Berlin-based electronic musician and DJ whose career spans over three decades. Originally from Chile, he has been a pivotal figure in the Berlin electronic music scene, co-founding the legendary label Shitkatapult and being involved in various projects bridging Latin American and European electronic music traditions. His live performances are known for their eclectic mix of dub, techno, electro, and experimental sounds, delivered through a carefully curated hardware setup.

## The Live Setup

**Benjamin Weiss:** What is your live setup?

**Jammin Unit:** Over the years my setup has changed a lot, but the philosophy has stayed the same — I want machines that I can play like instruments. Right now I use a combination of grooveboxes, a sampler, and a mixer with built-in effects. I sequence everything from hardware — no laptop. The key is that every piece of gear has to earn its place. If I can't use it intuitively and spontaneously, it doesn't come to the gig.

I also always have a small analog synth for basslines and a drum machine. The mixer is very important — it's where I shape the sound and create the dynamics of the set.

## Dub Techniques and Live Mixing

**Benjamin Weiss:** Dub seems to be a big influence on your live approach.

**Jammin Unit:** Absolutely. The mixer is my main instrument. I use the EQ, the sends, and the faders all the time — cutting frequencies, sending things into delay and reverb, creating drops and builds. That's the dub approach: the mix is the performance. You take the same elements and completely transform them just through mixing and effects.

I grew up listening to dub and reggae, and that way of thinking about music — as something spatial, something you sculpt in real time — has always been at the core of what I do.

## Cultural Connections

**Benjamin Weiss:** How does your Chilean background influence your electronic music?

**Jammin Unit:** Latin American music has such rich rhythmic traditions. Cumbia, for example, has these hypnotic, cyclical rhythms that work incredibly well in an electronic context. I've explored that a lot — taking traditional rhythmic patterns and reinterpreting them with electronic instruments. It's not about making "world music" — it's about recognizing that rhythm is universal and these traditions have a lot to teach us about groove and movement.

Berlin has been my home for decades, and the exchange between cultures here has been essential for my work. That cross-pollination of ideas is what keeps electronic music interesting.

## What's Missing

**Benjamin Weiss:** Is there gear you wish existed?

**Jammin Unit:** A really good, compact dub delay with hands-on control — something dedicated to live dub mixing. Most delays are designed for guitarists or for studio use. I want something that's optimized for the kind of real-time manipulation you do in a dub-style live set. Big knobs, responsive feedback control, maybe a spring reverb built in.

I'd also love a hardware device that can do real-time time-stretching of loops without artifacts. That would open up so many possibilities for live performance.

## Feedback on the TBD-Toolkit Prototype

**Benjamin Weiss:** What would you do with the TBD prototype?

**Jammin Unit:** Dub effects — that's the first thing. A really good tape-style delay with controllable feedback and filtering. But also a looper where I can capture moments from the set and manipulate them. If it could do both of those things well, it would become a permanent part of my setup.

The idea of an open platform is great because you can adapt it to your specific needs. Not everyone plays the same way, and having a device that can be configured for your particular workflow is very valuable.
