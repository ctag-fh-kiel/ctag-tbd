**JakoJako** is a London-based electronic musician, producer, and DJ whose work spans club music, experimental electronics, and sound art. Her productions and live sets draw on a wide palette of influences — from deconstructed club to noise to ambient — blending intricate rhythmic programming with raw, physical sound design. She performs with a hybrid setup combining hardware and software, and has played at festivals and venues across the UK and Europe.

## The Live Setup

**Benjamin Weiss:** What is your live setup?

**JakoJako:** It changes quite a lot depending on the context, but the core is always a laptop running Ableton Live, a hardware controller, and a couple of synths. I like having the flexibility of software for sequencing and effects processing, but I need hardware for the tactile element — I need knobs and faders I can grab.

For drums and percussion, I often use a sample-based approach with a lot of processing on top. I also bring a small analog synth for basslines and textures. Effects-wise, I run a lot through hardware — a tape delay and a reverb pedal are usually in the chain.

## Improvisation and Structure

**Benjamin Weiss:** How do you balance preparation and improvisation?

**JakoJako:** I prepare stems and loops, but the arrangement is live. I decide in the moment which elements to bring in and how to layer them. The transitions and builds are all real-time decisions. I think of it more like DJing with my own material — I have all these pieces and I'm assembling them on the fly.

What I enjoy most is the moments where things go somewhere unexpected. When you layer two things together and they create something you didn't plan — that's the magic of live performance.

## Sound Design Philosophy

**Benjamin Weiss:** How would you describe your approach to sound design?

**JakoJako:** I'm attracted to sounds that feel physical — sounds with weight and texture. I spend a lot of time processing field recordings, found sounds, and synthesizer patches through chains of effects until they become something unrecognizable. I like when a sound has a history, when you can feel that it went through a journey to get to where it is.

Rhythm is also central to everything I do. Even my ambient work has an underlying pulse. I think about rhythm in a very physical way — how it makes the body move, how it creates tension and release.

## What's Missing

**Benjamin Weiss:** Is there anything you wish existed for live performance?

**JakoJako:** A really good hardware granular sampler with an intuitive interface. Something where you can load in audio and immediately start manipulating it in interesting ways without diving into menus. Also, better tools for incorporating generative elements into a live set in a way that you can still steer and control.

I'm also always looking for better ways to process audio in real time. Multi-effects units exist, but they're often either too simple or too complex. Something in between — powerful but immediately playable — that's what I want.

## Feedback on the TBD-Toolkit Prototype

**Benjamin Weiss:** What would you do with the TBD prototype?

**JakoJako:** I'd want to use it as a multi-effects processor first — something I can route audio through and manipulate with the physical controls. Granular effects, spectral processing, creative delays. But I'd also be interested in using it as a sound source — a synthesizer with unusual architectures that I can't get from off-the-shelf gear.

The open-source aspect is really compelling. Being able to create exactly the tool you need for your specific performance practice — that's powerful.
