# The Live Setup: Form Factor and Physicality

**Benjamin Weiss:** You play live a lot. What’s important to you about the gear you use on stage? What do you usually play with?

**JakoJako:** It always depends on the setting. If I’m playing ambient, I use polysynths and modular systems—always a combination of external sequencers and the internal sequencers of the devices. If I’m playing a techno set, I swap the polysynth for drum machines.

The most important thing to me is the form factor. It has to be small. That’s why I use the modular system—it packs so many voices and so much diversity into a relatively small footprint that it never gets boring. Everything has to fit into one large case; it shouldn’t be bigger than the largest Pelican case. I don’t want to deal with a big Pelican case, a modular system, and then carry my clothes on my back without a tour manager. So: the smaller, the better, and ideally, more functions in one device.

If a device has multiple functions, like those DSP-based things, it’s important to be able to switch fluidly. While the clock is running, I want to be able to change modes and have the system shift seamlessly. If I’m using samplers or polysynths, I want to be able to change presets without it stopping. It just needs to keep running without me having to press reset or stop.

But it can’t be some cheap plastic thing like the [1010music Fireball](https://1010music.com/product/fireball). That thing just flies around. I don’t have a fixed board with Velcro, and touchscreens often stop working in clubs when things get sweaty. It can’t be too small or cheap; it has to stand solidly. Like Waldorf or Elektron gear, which is made of metal and heavier.

I take the [Waldorf Iridium Core](https://waldorfmusic.com/de/produkt/iridium-core/) with me everywhere now. They designed the format after asking what could be improved—the size now matches the Elektron devices exactly, so it fits in the case. It sounds big but is small.

## Haptics and Performance Factors

**JakoJako:** The feel of the knobs and faders is important to me. When it’s dark, smoky, and foggy, you need to feel resistance. Not everything should be an endless encoder with LEDs; I need real knobs with resistance.

Then there’s the performance factor: being able to remember things, having a button that can map multiple functions. For example, with the Iridium, I wanted these ABCD buttons at the bottom that can be assigned as macro controls. They should be able to do multiple things at once, ideally for transitions.

Compatibility is also key. If MIDI suddenly stops working during a performance but you need a clock, it’s good to have a clock output on the back that you can plug in with a standard patch cable. Uncomplicated, but with as many options as possible: USB MIDI, MIDI over TRS, a MIDI thru for emergencies. It happens relatively often that something suddenly stops working, and you need to react quickly.

And it has to be loud enough. All those OP-Z or OP-1 things are quiet. It’s a fart; no one can use it. There’s more noise than anything else. I once saw Stimming—he had all these 1010music parts, which are loud and don’t make noise. But he had the subs under the stage and just threw the little things into his backpack after the set. Not ideal either.

## Backup Systems and Improvisation

**Benjamin Weiss:** Do you have a fallback system if your setup fails?

**JakoJako:** If it really fails, I have to play a DJ set. I had that happen in Japan at the beginning of the year. I had built an entire set around this pumping forest sound, but the entire end-of-chain wasn’t working. Probably a power supply that didn’t work in Japan. We messed around for two hours, someone organized another compressor, but I couldn’t play my set as planned. The whole aesthetic was built around everything pumping and breathing beautifully at the end. Without that, it sounded like crap, everything was too open. I felt really bad because they had flown me in specifically. So I played a DJ set and did a bit of ambient with reverb and polysynths the next day.

My fallback system is the DJ set. I’d be ashamed to just run playback. I’d rather say, “Sorry, it’s broken, but I’ll offer you something else.”

**Benjamin Weiss:** How much do you improvise with your sequences?

**JakoJako:** If it’s ambient, I play with the filter and switch the oscillators. The main focus is on the transitions so it’s not always the same trick with the reverb. In techno, I have the main elements like kicks prepared as sequences and do the synthesizers live. The focus is on the drums to keep everything running, and I have my hands free for those weird sounds in between.

## Wishes: The Sequence Crossfader and Loop Stations

**Benjamin Weiss:** Is there a device you’ve always wanted but haven’t found yet?

**JakoJako:** I’m looking for something specific. The Octatrack has this audio crossfader. I wonder why that doesn’t exist for sequences. You could have one sequence in A and one in B and crossfade between them. You could strip notes proportionally so that as you move the crossfader, only the note that fits both patterns keeps playing. The melodies would morph musically instead of just switching abruptly. That doesn’t exist yet.

Loop stations have also never really worked for me. There’s the Boss looper and all sorts of things, but they’re either too primitive, the audio quality isn’t good, or the workflow is never great. It’s not intuitive. Either it’s not really on the one when you press record, or it looks like that Boss pedal made of plastic, which is more for singer-songwriters with guitars. I’m missing an intuitive electronic looper. The Octatrack is too fiddly for me; I’m scared of pressing the wrong button and overwriting everything.

The [1010music Blackbox](https://1010music.com/product/blackbox) does it pretty well, but again, there’s that touch display. I can’t switch functions in the pads without getting confused. A small, cool looper would be nice.

## Algorithms and Visual Feedback

**Benjamin Weiss:** Are you interested in algorithmic sequencers?

**JakoJako:** Yes, like the OXI One. But my biggest interest is in sequencers like the HAPAX because it can run two projects simultaneously. You can smoothly transition from A to B. That way, I could have my ambient set and my techno set open at the same time and just pull over a sequence from 2021. But again, it’s a small screen that you really have to learn.

I also like the algorithmic creation of melodies, like with a shift register. The one in [Ornament & Crime](https://ornament-and-cri.me/) is probably still the coolest, producing the nicest melodies. There are also things from Cubit, but they often don’t sound as musical.

I like the visual feedback from [Euclidean Circles](https://vpme.de/euclidean-circles/) by VPME. You see the circle and how the notes shift. With other devices, you just look at the screen and see numbers, which doesn’t make sense. When you turn the knob and the circle fills up, you know: the intensity is increasing.

I actually don’t need a display at all. With a modular system, you focus on how it sounds and feels, not on parameters. You remember the positions—muscle memory. Often, a screen is even a turn-off. I love the Waldorf Iridium for its functions, but it does look like a controller.

## Feedback on the TBD-Toolkit

**Benjamin Weiss:** [Shows the prototype] What would you do with this “canvas”?

**JakoJako:** I’d make a sequencer out of it, a MIDI looper. It feels nice. If it were DSP-based, I’d try to fit my live set into it: a drum machine mode and a poly-synth mode. If both could run simultaneously, you could split it: drums here, chord progressions there. That way, you could build song structures.

It would also be a great transition tool. A buffer with effects and a looper, a kind of multi-effects unit for glitch textures. I don’t need much, really just a reverb, but for transitions, it’s cool to load signals and do granular stuff.

**Benjamin Weiss:** How many voices would you need for the drum machine?

**JakoJako:** Kick, bass, tom, clap, two hi-hats, snare, and a sample slot. With eight voices, you’re already really well served. If I had all that on one small device, I could travel with the modular system in my carry-on. That would be much less stress when checking in.

An algorithmic sequencer that branches off melodies and creates generative new material would interest me the most. Keeping the main notes but changing the rhythm coherently. That way, you come up with new ideas when jamming live for eight hours and don’t always pull out the same tricks.
