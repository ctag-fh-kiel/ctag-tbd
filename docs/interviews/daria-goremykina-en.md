**Daria Goremykina** is a Berlin-based electronic musician, sound artist, and DJ originally from Russia. Known for her deeply textural and immersive live sets, she combines hardware synthesizers, drum machines, and modular gear to create hypnotic, evolving soundscapes that blur the lines between techno, ambient, and experimental music. Her performances have taken her to clubs and festivals across Europe.

## The Live Setup

**Benjamin Weiss:** What is your live setup?

**Daria Goremykina:** My setup is quite compact — I use a combination of a drum machine, a couple of synthesizers, and effects pedals. I like to keep things minimal so I can focus on the sound rather than the gear. I have a groovebox for rhythmic elements and sequencing, a mono synth for basslines and leads, and then I process everything through a chain of effects — mainly delay, reverb, and distortion.

I also use a small mixer to blend everything together and have control over the levels during the performance. Everything runs through a single stereo output, which makes it easy to set up at any venue.

## Preparation and Improvisation

**Benjamin Weiss:** How much do you prepare before a live set?

**Daria Goremykina:** I prepare the sounds and patches beforehand — I spend a lot of time designing the timbres I want to use. But the actual arrangement and structure is completely improvised. I have a rough idea of how I want to start and where I want to go, but the path between those points is always different.

I think that's what makes live performance exciting — you're responding to the room, the audience, the energy. If something unexpected happens, you follow it. That's much more interesting than playing back a pre-arranged set.

## Sound Design and Textures

**Benjamin Weiss:** What role does sound design play in your live work?

**Daria Goremykina:** It's everything. The textures and timbres are what define the mood and atmosphere of a set. I spend a lot of time layering sounds, finding interesting harmonic relationships, and creating movement through modulation. I'm drawn to sounds that have a lot of internal complexity — sounds that evolve and shift over time.

Effects are a big part of that too. I use long reverb tails and feedback delays to create a sense of space and depth. It's about building an environment that the audience can get lost in.

## What's Missing

**Benjamin Weiss:** Is there anything you've been looking for that you haven't found yet?

**Daria Goremykina:** I'd love to have a compact device that can do granular processing in real time with a hands-on interface. There are software solutions, but I want something I can touch and manipulate physically during a performance. Also, a really good live looper with multiple layers and easy undo would be amazing.

I'm also interested in spatial audio — being able to move sounds around in a three-dimensional space during a live set. That's something I think will become more important as venues start to adopt immersive sound systems.

## Feedback on the TBD-Toolkit Prototype

**Benjamin Weiss:** What would you do with the TBD prototype?

**Daria Goremykina:** First thing — granular effects and a live looper. Those would be my top priorities. But I can also see it being great as a sample player with lots of modulation options, or as a multi-effects processor. The fact that it's open and programmable is really appealing. You could adapt it to whatever your set needs at that moment.

I'd also be curious about using it for generative sequencing — having it create patterns and variations that I can guide and shape in real time. That kind of controlled randomness can add a lot of life to a performance.
